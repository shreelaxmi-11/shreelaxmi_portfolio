01 Feature Overview

What Apple shipped

Apple Notes transcription has two distinct layers. The first is SpeechAnalyzer a new ASR (automatic speech recognition) API introduced in iOS 26 / macOS Tahoe, replacing the older SFSpeechRecognizer from iOS 10. It runs entirely on-device, handles long-form audio (lectures, meetings, calls), and powers Notes, Voice Memos, Journal, and now third-party apps. The second is Apple Intelligence summarization which takes a completed transcript and generates a summary using the on-device LLM (or Private Cloud Compute for more complex requests).

These two layers are independently powerful but create interesting friction when combined. The teardown evaluates both.

02 What Works Well

Three things Apple got right

On-device speed & privacy
Works well

SpeechAnalyzer processed a 34-minute 7GB video in 45 seconds in benchmark testing 55% faster than OpenAI's Whisper Large-v3 Turbo model, with no reported quality difference. This is the right technical bet: inference entirely on Apple Silicon, zero round-trip latency, zero audio transmitted externally. For a platform company that markets privacy as a core value proposition, shipping a best-in-class speed number on-device is a meaningful win.

The model is stored in a system-wide asset catalog shared across all apps zero app size impact. If any app has already downloaded the model, a newly installed app skips the download entirely. This is thoughtful infrastructure design that reduces the "first run friction" that typically kills adoption of on-device AI features.

Source: MacRumors benchmark, WWDC25 SpeechAnalyzer session
Shortcuts + AI pipeline integration
Works well

Apple opened the SpeechAnalyzer API to third-party developers in iOS 26, and the integration with Shortcuts is genuinely powerful. Users can build automations like: record a lecture → transcribe → compare to existing notes → extract missed key points. This makes transcription a composable building block rather than a dead-end Notes feature. No competitor offers this level of on-device automation chaining for audio.

The first-party examples (Notes + Voice Memos + Journal) also show Apple is eating its own cooking the same API developers use is the one powering system apps, which is a credibility signal for the developer ecosystem.

Source: Apple Newsroom, WWDC25
Modular API architecture
Works well

SpeechAnalyzer uses a module-based design developers attach a SpeechTranscriber module or SpeechDetector module to an analyzer session independently. Modules can be added or removed dynamically mid-session. This is a significant improvement over SFSpeechRecognizer's monolithic design, which forced all-or-nothing configuration. The Swift-native async/await design also makes it easier to build real-time streaming transcription UIs without complex callback chains.

Source: Apple Developer Documentation, WWDC25 session 277
03 Real Gaps

Where the product falls short

Language locked to device language
Critical gap

Transcription only works in the language the device is set to. A Colombian user living in Germany with their phone set to English cannot transcribe a German-language lecture they must change their entire device language, which affects Siri, keyboard, and system UI. This is a fundamental UX failure for multilingual users, and Apple community forums are full of frustrated reports about this exact issue.

Samsung Transcript Assist handles this cleanly: users choose the recording language independently of device language at transcription time. Apple's design forces a global setting change for what should be a per-recording preference.

Source: Apple Community forums, multiple threads Sept–Oct 2025 Source: Apple Support device language requirement confirmed
No speaker diarization in Notes
Critical gap

Apple Notes transcription does not identify or label individual speakers. For the primary use case recording a meeting or lecture this means the entire transcript is a flat monologue with no way to know who said what. Samsung Transcript Assist labels speakers by contact name. Google Recorder labels speakers generically (Speaker 1, Speaker 2). Apple has no speaker attribution at all in the Notes flow.

Notably, the SpeechAnalyzer API itself does not expose speaker diarization as a capability this isn't a missing UI element, it's a missing model capability. Argmax's competing WhisperKit does offer speaker recognition. This is a gap at the infrastructure level, not just the product layer.

Source: Argmax SpeechAnalyzer vs WhisperKit comparison
Long recording failures
Reliability gap

Recordings of 45–60 minutes or longer frequently fail to transcribe, fail to export, or silently disappear from Notes. This is a documented bug across Apple community forums with multiple reports from journalists, researchers, and academics exactly the power users who most need long-form transcription. Users describe workarounds like setting timers to restart recording every 30 minutes, or screen-recording the playback to capture audio.

A feature positioned as a productivity tool for meetings and lectures that fails on meetings longer than 45 minutes has a fundamental product-market fit problem. The core use case is broken for the users with the highest need.

Source: Apple Community forums, multiple threads 2024–2025
No custom vocabulary
Partial gap

SpeechAnalyzer dropped custom vocabulary support that existed in the older SFSpeechRecognizer. This matters for domain-specific use cases medical terminology, legal jargon, company-specific product names, or even a podcast show name like "AppStories" (reported to be transcribed as two words in early tests). Argmax's analysis confirms SpeechAnalyzer is measurably less accurate than SFSpeechRecognizer on custom keywords, and their WhisperKit significantly outperforms both with custom vocabulary enabled.

For general consumer use this is minor. For enterprise or professional segments (doctors, lawyers, journalists), it's a dealbreaker that keeps them on third-party tools.

Source: Argmax blog, SpeechAnalyzer vs WhisperKit comparison

04 Prioritized Fixes

If I owned this feature

Ranked by user impact × technical feasibility. Not wishlist these are changes a PM with engineering budget could ship in 2–3 quarters.

01
Per-recording language selection
Decouple transcription language from device language. Add a language picker at the point of transcription (not recording) matching Samsung's UX. The SpeechAnalyzer API already accepts a locale parameter; this is a UI and product decision, not an ML problem. No new model training required. Direct fix for the most-reported user complaint in the Apple forums, and a win for every multilingual user globally.
High impact · Low effort
02
Long recording chunking & reliability
Implement automatic chunking for recordings over 30 minutes split into overlapping segments, transcribe independently, stitch with deduplication. This is a known engineering pattern (used by Whisper-based tools) and resolves the most critical reliability gap. Also fix the export bug for large files separately as a hotfix. A productivity feature that silently eats an hour-long interview is a trust-destroying experience.
High impact · Medium effort
03
Speaker diarization in Notes
Add speaker segmentation to the SpeechAnalyzer model at minimum generic "Speaker A / Speaker B" labels, with a second phase matching against Contacts for named attribution (as Samsung does). This requires model-level work and is a larger investment, but it's the single feature that makes the difference between a student transcript tool and a professional meeting tool. The Contacts matching is a meaningful privacy differentiator all on-device, no cloud lookup.
High impact · High effort
05 PM Lens

Strategic observations

The infrastructure is exceptional; the product layer is unfinished
SpeechAnalyzer is genuinely impressive engineering fastest on-device ASR available, modular, open to developers, zero storage overhead. The product built on top of it in Notes is significantly behind. Apple shipped a Ferrari engine with a bicycle frame. The fixes required are largely product and UX decisions, not new ML work.
Apple is winning on platform, not product
The Shortcuts integration and third-party developer API are the real strategic moves here not the Notes feature itself. Apple is positioning SpeechAnalyzer as an ecosystem capability that developers build on, not a terminal consumer feature. This is the right long-term bet, but it means the first-party Notes experience suffers from underinvestment while platform is prioritized.
The language gap is a global market problem
Tying transcription to device language means Apple's feature effectively doesn't work for hundreds of millions of multilingual users expats, international students, global business travelers. Samsung's 22-language support isn't just a feature count competition; it's a meaningful market reach advantage. Apple's device-language constraint is a product choice masquerading as a technical constraint.
Reliability is a prerequisite, not a nice-to-have
A journalist who loses an hour-long interview because Notes silently failed to transcribe it will not use the feature again. The long recording failure bug is category-defining: it is the primary use case for which the feature was marketed. Shipping a 45-minute cliff-edge on a feature positioned for "meetings and lectures" suggests the feature shipped before adequate real-world testing. Fix this before adding any new capabilities.
